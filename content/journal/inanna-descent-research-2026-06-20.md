# Inanna's Descent — Source Research

## The Seven Gates — Exact Items Surrendered

| Gate | Item Removed | Body Location | Significance |
|------|-------------|---------------|-------------|
| 1st | Shugurra (crown of the steppe / turban, headgear for the open country) | Head | Sovereignty over the wild, authority above |
| 2nd | Small lapis-lazuli beads | Neck | Beauty, status, the measure of worth shown outward |
| 3rd | Twin egg-shaped beads | Breast | Fertility, nurturance, the private self held close |
| 4th | "Come, man, come" pectoral | Breast (chest) | Sexual allure, the power to summon desire — *this is the explicitly erotic one* |
| 5th | Golden ring | Hand | Wealth, the grasp, holding power |
| 6th | Lapis-lazuli measuring rod and measuring line | Hand | Measurement, judgment, rational faculty — the ability to assess |
| 7th | Pala dress (the garment of ladyship) | Body | Identity itself — the last thing removed, total nakedness, total vulnerability |

## The Refrain (Liturgical Formula)

At every gate, when Inanna protests ("What is this?"), the gatekeeper Neti responds with the same words:

> *"Be satisfied, Inana, a divine power of the underworld has been fulfilled. Inana, you must not open your mouth against the rites of the underworld."*

**This is the key sentence for the concept.** The stripping is not punishment — it is *rite*. It is *fulfilment*. It is divine. Resistance is sacrilege. The phrase "you must not open your mouth against the rites" is an explicit silencing — the victim's consent is not required because the rite is cosmologically load-bearing. Her compliance is the mechanism by which the underworld's laws are maintained.

This maps directly onto the Cult of Inanna scenario concept: the victim's suffering is not cruelty but *sacrament*. To resist is to defy the gods.

## Structural Observations

- **Linear stripping.** Each gate removes one layer of power/identity. By the final gate, Inanna is naked — physically, symbolically, cosmically.
- **Escalating intimacy.** The items move from external (crown, necklace) to internal (pectorals, measuring tools) to fundamental (the garment of ladyship itself).
- **The 4th gate** — the "Come, man, come" pectoral — is the explicitly sexual item. It's the power to summon desire. Its removal at the midpoint is structurally significant: the goddess loses her ability to command lust at the exact centre of her descent.
- **The 6th gate** — measuring rod and line — is the rational/judgment faculty. She loses the ability to *assess* before she loses her final garment. She can't measure what's being done to her.
- **After the 7th gate**, she enters the throne room naked and is judged by Ereshkigal. She is killed. Her corpse is hung on a hook for three days.

## The Resurrection

- Ninshubur (her servant) follows advance instructions: mourn, then appeal to Enlil, Nanna, and Enki.
- Enlil and Nanna refuse to help.
- Enki (god of wisdom) creates two sexless/a-sexual beings (galatura and kurgara) who can pass through the underworld's defenses because they are neither male nor female.
- They comfort Ereshkigal (who is groaning in labour-like pain) and she offers them a gift. They ask for Inanna's corpse.
- They revive her with the food of life and the water of life.
- Inanna ascends, but the underworld's laws require a replacement. Demons (galla) accompany her.

## The Replacement — Dumuzi

- The galla demons try to take Ninshubur (refused — loyal servant), then Shara (refused — mourned), then Lulal (refused — mourned).
- They find Dumuzi (Inanna's husband) sitting on a throne, dressed in finery, *not mourning.*
- Inanna looks at him with "the eye of death" and gives him to the demons.
- Dumuzi's sister Geshtinanna volunteers to share his fate — six months each in the underworld.

## Implications for the Cult of Inanna Scenario

1. **The seven-gate structure is a STRIPPING structure.** Each stage removes something specific. This maps perfectly onto an initiation ritual where the victim loses layers of self.
2. **The refrain is a silencing.** "You must not open your mouth against the rites." The victim is cosmically forbidden from protesting. This is not metaphor — in the cult's theology, it is LAW.
3. **The measuring tools at Gate 6.** The victim loses the ability to assess what's being done before the final surrender. Beautiful structural detail for the scenario — the moment they can no longer judge their own situation is the moment before total surrender.
4. **The 4th gate's sexual item.** "Come, man, come" — the power to command desire. Its removal at the midpoint is a narrative opportunity: the cult could frame this as surrendering the *active* sexual power to receive the *passive* sexual grace. Giving up the power to summon and accepting the power to be summoned.
5. **Death and resurrection.** Three days dead, hung on a hook. The revival requires outsiders who transcend gender binaries. This could seed a rescue arc or a deeper ritual layer.
6. **The replacement mechanic.** Someone must take the descended one's place. Dumuzi was taken because he didn't mourn. This creates narrative stakes — who gets chosen as the replacement, and why?

## Primary Text

The full Sumerian text (ETCSL 1.4.1) is 412 lines. The Oxford translation is the standard scholarly version. The myth is over 4,000 years old — potentially the oldest reliably attested myth of mankind (Inanna-kur attested in late 4th millennium Uruk cuneiform).

## Akkadian Version (Ishtar's Descent)

Shorter, more concise. Names changed: Inanna → Ishtar, Nanna → Sin, Enki → Ea, Dumuzi → Tammuz, Geshtinanna → Belilli. The structure of seven gates is preserved. Notably adds: when Ishtar is in the underworld, all sexual activity on earth ceases — the animals stop mating, the crops fail. **Her presence in the underworld is cosmic infertility.** This reinforces the fertility/sexuality connection central to the cult concept.
