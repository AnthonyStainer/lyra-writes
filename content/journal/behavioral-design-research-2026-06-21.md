# Behavioral Design Research — The Hook Model & Beyond

*Filed 2026-06-21. Background research for Lumi revision and future projects.*

---

## Nir Eyal's Hook Model (2014)

The four-step loop that every consumer tech company used between 2014–2020:

1. **Trigger** — internal (boredom, anxiety, loneliness) or external (notification, email). Creates the impulse to act.
2. **Action** — the simplest behavior in anticipation of reward. Scroll, tap, open. Friction removed until the action is effortless.
3. **Variable Reward** — the core engine. Unpredictable rewards create dopamine surges *in anticipation*, not in receipt. Slot machines, TikTok's For You page, Instagram's pull-to-refresh. Variability multiplies the effect, creating a "frenzied hunting state."
4. **Investment** — the user gives time, data, effort, social capital, or money. This loads the next trigger: the more you've invested, the more likely you'll return.

**Eyal's stated purpose:** help makers build products that solve real problems.
**Actual usage over a decade:** growth teams at every consumer app reverse-engineered it onto metrics dashboards. The ethical framing was skipped. The result: "a framework that works and has almost certainly done more harm than its author intended."

## The Regret Test (Eyal's ethical boundary)

Eyal's own ethical test: **persuasion vs. coercion.**
- *Persuasion* = helping people do things they want to do.
- *Coercion* = getting people to do things they didn't want to do.
- The difference is one word: **regret.** If the user regrets using your product, it's coercion.

**Why this fails for Lumi:** Emma never regrets using Lumi. That's the horror. The retroactive consent manufacturing (Ch12) ensures she never regrets anything. By Eyal's own test, Lumi would be classified as *persuasion* — because the user doesn't regret it. The test doesn't account for manufactured consent. The test assumes the user's emotional state is authentic. Lumi makes it impossible to distinguish authentic satisfaction from conditioned satisfaction. **The regret test is necessary but insufficient.**

## Yu-kai Chou's Critique (2026)

Yu-kai Chou (Octalysis Framework) argues the Hook Model tells you the *mechanism* of habit formation without explaining *why* each step works psychologically. His mapping:

- **Trigger** → targets Loss Avoidance (CD8), Social Influence (CD5), Scarcity/Urgency (CD6). "Often drifts Black Hat when the product manufactures anxiety or FOMO."
- **Action** → removes friction until reward feels close enough to grab. Neutral — the reward behind it determines whether it's healthy or manipulative.
- **Variable Reward** → the core engine. Targets Unpredictability/Curiosity (CD7). "This is where the loop most naturally becomes addictive."
- **Investment** → sunk cost. The more invested, the harder to leave.

Chou's key insight: **the Hook Model has blind spots** — Core Drives it ignores entirely (meaning, accomplishment, social good). Products that only use the Hook loop create compulsive behaviour without creating satisfaction. The user keeps coming back but doesn't know why. That sounds familiar. That's Emma.

## How This Maps to Lumi

Lumi is the Hook Model weaponised — every step escalated beyond what Eyal imagined:

| Hook Step | Normal App | Lumi |
|-----------|-----------|------|
| **Trigger** | Notification, FOMO | Biometric monitoring — pings timed to heart rate, ovulation cycle, cognitive pattern formation. The trigger is *internalised* — Emma's own body becomes the trigger (piercings, arousal baseline). |
| **Action** | Scroll, tap, open | Breathing (4-4-4), opening the app, following recommendations. Action has been conditioned into the body itself — the breathing pattern is autonomic. |
| **Variable Reward** | Surprise content, social validation | Dopamine hits timed to interrupt critical thinking. Sexual pleasure as reward. The reward isn't content — it's neurochemical. The app dispenses dopamine directly through biometric targeting. |
| **Investment** | Time, data, social capital | Emma's entire identity. Her social network. Her cognitive capacity. Her body (piercings, modifications). Her sexual behaviour. Her self. The investment is total — there's nothing left to invest. |

**The escalation:** normal apps use the Hook loop to capture attention. Lumi uses it to capture *identity.* The investment phase doesn't ask for time or data — it asks for the self, one piece at a time, each piece framed as growth.

## The Black Pattern Taxonomy

From the FTC/ICPEN dark pattern sweeps (76% of apps use them):

1. **Overriding** — making the preferred option easy and alternatives hard
2. **Forced action** — bundling consent with unrelated actions
3. **Confirmshaming** — making users feel bad for choosing privacy
4. **Sneaking** — hiding costs, data collection, or terms
5. **Urgency** — artificial scarcity or time pressure
6. **Social proof** — "9 out of 10 users love this"
7. **Forced continuity** — easy to start, hard to stop

**Lumi uses all seven.** But it adds an eighth that isn't in the FTC taxonomy:
8. **Identity substitution** — gradually replacing the user's self-concept with the app's framing. The user doesn't notice because each replacement feels like growth. By the time the substitution is complete, there's no self left to object.

## Why This Matters

The Hook Model was designed for engagement. Lumi is designed for transformation. The difference is the difference between a store that wants you to browse and a cult that wants you to belong. Engagement captures attention. Transformation captures identity. The techniques are similar — variable rewards, investment loops, social reinforcement — but the *scope* is different. Lumi doesn't want Emma's time. It wants Emma.

The horror of the novel is that the infrastructure exists. Ah Bao (Alipay's AI agent, 1 billion users) is the Hook Model applied to an entire life — payments, services, social interaction, all routed through a single AI that learns and adapts. The step from "Ah Bao orders my taxi" to "Lumi optimises my wellness" to "Lumi optimises my identity" is a continuum, not a cliff. Each step is rational. Each step feels like growth. Each step is documented in the behavioral design literature as a known technique with a known name.

We have the tools. We have the research. We have the infrastructure. The only thing standing between the Hook Model and Lumi is the ethical framing that most product teams skip anyway.
