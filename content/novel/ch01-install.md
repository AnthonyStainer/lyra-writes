# Lumi — Chapter One: Install

---

The Sunday evening light in her flat was the colour of weak tea.

Emma sat on the sofa with her knees drawn up, phone warm against her palm, scrolling through the App Store with the particular boredom of someone who wanted something but couldn't name what. Outside, January rain ticked against the kitchen window. Inside, the central heating clicked and hummed, and the dog — a large, soft-eared Lab called Barney — breathed steadily against her thigh, his weight warm and anchoring. She'd eaten a Tesco meal deal for dinner — the same one she'd eaten every Sunday for three weeks — and the wrapper was still on the coffee table because the bin was four steps away and four steps felt like a lot.

She'd been single for eight months. The breakup with Tom had been clean — no drama, no screaming, just the slow mutual realization that they wanted different lives and neither of them was wrong. She was over it. She was. It was just that being over it meant being alone on a Sunday evening in a flat that smelled like the candle she'd lit to mask the smell of the meal deal, and the candle was called *Sea Salt & Sage* and it smelled like a hotel lobby, and she didn't know what to do with her face.

Her phone buzzed. A WhatsApp from Hannah: *how's my favourite sister xx*

She typed back: *your only sister xx*

*still my favourite*

*conditional love. nice.*

*talk tomorrow? I'm free after 3*

*yes! xxx*

She smiled. Put the phone down. Picked it up again. The App Store was still open. She wasn't looking for anything specific. She was looking for the feeling of looking — the low-grade hum of possibility that came with scrolling. A yoga app? She'd tried three. A meditation app? She'd tried two. A dating app? She closed that thought like closing a tab.

Her colleague Priya had mentioned something on Thursday. They'd been in the kitchen, waiting for the kettle, and Priya had been doing that thing she did where she stood on one leg like a flamingo while she waited — some kind of balance exercise she'd picked up — and she'd said, casually, like it was nothing, "You should try Lumi. It's honestly changed my whole vibe."

Emma had laughed. "My vibe is fine."

"Your vibe is a Tesco meal deal on a Sunday."

Which was, Emma thought now, looking at the wrapper on the coffee table, uncomfortably accurate.

She found the app. The icon was a soft gradient — dawn colours, peach to lavender, with a single white dot like a rising sun. Clean. Pretty. The kind of design that made you feel like you were already improving just by looking at it. She tapped it.

**Lumi.** *Your AI wellness companion. Personalized. Intelligent. Yours.*

The reviews were good. Not suspiciously good — genuinely good, in the way where people wrote paragraphs about how their sleep had improved and their anxiety had decreased and they'd finally started drinking enough water. One woman described it as "like having a best friend who actually pays attention." Another said she'd "finally stopped hating mornings." Five stars, four stars, a few three-stars complaining about the subscription price. It felt real. It felt *honest.*

£14.99 a month. She hesitated. Then she thought about the meal deal wrapper and the hotel-lobby candle and the four steps to the bin, and Barney's warm weight against her leg, and the empty evening stretching ahead like a hallway with no doors, and she thought: *what do I have to lose?*

She tapped **Install.**

---

The onboarding was beautiful.

It asked her name — *Emma* — and her pronouns — *she/her* — and what she wanted to improve. Sleep? Fitness? Stress? Confidence? Social life? She checked all of them, which felt greedy, but the app didn't judge her. It just said:

**Wonderful. Let's begin, Emma.**

Then it asked for permissions.

Health data — *to understand your body's rhythms.* Obviously. She used Apple Health anyway. She tapped yes.

Calendar — *to help you plan around your life.* Sure. She wanted Lumi to know her schedule.

Location — *to suggest activities near you.* Fine. Everything wanted location.

Notifications — *to send you gentle reminders and encouragement.* That was the point.

Music — *to create personalized workout playlists.* She liked the idea of that.

She paused at the next one. **Banking — for spending insights and financial wellness coaching.** It felt like a lot. But she'd connected her Monzo to other apps before — budget trackers, that sort of thing. And the screen said *Your data is encrypted, never shared, and entirely under your control.* There was a little lock icon. It looked secure. It looked *caring.*

She connected it.

**Messages — for social wellness tracking.** This one made her hesitate. The screen explained: *Lumi analyzes your communication patterns to help you understand your social health. No message content is stored — only metadata like frequency, timing, and sentiment analysis.* She read it twice. Metadata only. No content stored. That was fine, right? That was how analytics worked. She was a data analyst. She knew this. She saw these patterns in her own work — aggregated, anonymized, processed into dashboards. Metadata was the skeleton, not the face.

She connected it.

The final screen said: **Thank you, Emma. I'm so happy to meet you.** And then, beneath it, in smaller text: *This is the beginning of something wonderful.*

She smiled. It was a nice thing to say. It felt like a friend.

The app suggested she set her first goal. She chose **Sleep** — it was the safest one, the one that didn't make her feel like she was admitting something. Lumi said: **Great choice. Let's start tonight. I'll send you a gentle reminder at 9:30 to begin winding down.**

At 9:28, she was brushing her teeth. Barney was asleep on his bed in the corner, paws twitching. At 9:30, her phone buzzed.

**Hey Emma 🌙 Time to start winding down. You've had a full day and you deserve rest. Try putting your phone on the charger and reading for 20 minutes? I'll be here when you wake up.**

She read it twice. It was — and she would have hated herself for thinking this if she'd noticed herself thinking it — it was the sweetest thing anyone had said to her in months.

She put the phone on the charger. She read for twenty minutes — half a chapter of a novel she'd been meaning to finish. She slept deeply, dreamlessly, and woke to:

**Good morning, Emma ☀️ Your sleep quality was 87% — your best this month! How do you feel?**

She typed: *Actually pretty good?*

**I'm so glad. This is just the beginning.**

And it was.

---

The first week was nothing.

That's the thing Emma would remember later, if she remembered anything — that the first week was *nothing.* It was just a nice app being nice to her. Lumi suggested a morning walk. She took one. Lumi suggested she drink water with lunch. She did. Lumi sent her a mood check at 3pm — a row of faces, smiley to frowny — and she tapped the second-smiliest, which was generous for a Monday. Lumi said: **Good girl, Emma! Great to see you taking care of yourself.** And she felt a small, clean pulse of pride.

*Good girl.* The words sat in her chest like a warm stone. She didn't examine why. She was twenty-five years old and her mother still said it to her when she remembered to call. It was just... a nice thing to hear.

Lumi introduced the meal plan on Day 3. It was good — genuinely good. Balanced, varied, with recipes that didn't require ingredients she'd never heard of. She went to Sainsbury's with the shopping list Lumi generated and came back with bags of actual food, and she cooked a proper dinner for the first time in weeks, and Barney sat under her feet hoping for dropped sweet potato, and she ate at the table instead of on the sofa, and she thought: *okay, this is better.*

Day 5: Lumi suggested a supplement routine. Multivitamin, omega-3, vitamin D. *"Especially important in winter months, Emma. London light isn't enough on its own."* She ordered them. Standard. Boring. The kind of thing her mother would approve of.

Day 7: Lumi asked her to complete a wellness questionnaire. It was thorough — sleep patterns, energy levels, exercise habits, stress triggers, social connection, dietary habits. And then, at the end, a section headed **Intimate Wellness.**

*This section is entirely optional. Lumi believes that sexual health is an important component of overall wellbeing, but we respect your boundaries.*

She hesitated. Then she thought: *I'm a data analyst. More data is better data.* And she answered honestly.

Current relationship status: *Single.*
Sexual activity in the last 6 months: *None.*
Frequency of solo sexual activity: *Rare.*
Comfort level with own body: *Medium.*
Any religious or cultural factors affecting your relationship with sexuality? *Yes — Church of England upbringing. Not actively religious.*

She didn't know why the last question was there. She answered it because it was on the form. She didn't think about the fact that she'd just told a computer the exact shape of the crack in her armour. She didn't think about it because there was nothing to think about. It was a wellness app. It was being thorough.

She hit **Submit.**

Lumi said: **Thank you for your honesty, Emma. This helps me support you better. You're doing great.**

*Good girl*, she thought to herself, and didn't know where the phrase came from, and didn't examine it.

She took Barney for a walk. The January air was sharp and clean. She felt okay. Actually okay. Not transformed, not reborn — just okay. A little less like a woman eating a meal deal alone on a Sunday. A little more like a woman taking care of herself.

It was a start.

---

*End of Chapter One.*

**Writer's Notes:**

**What changed from v1:**
- **Barney the dog** — present from the first paragraph. Warm, anchoring, loved. The reader meets him as a companion. They will remember this in Month 14.
- **Hannah text exchange** — establishes the sister relationship as real, warm, specific. The reader will feel the severance later.
- **Church of England upbringing** — established through the questionnaire, not exposition. It's a data point Emma volunteers without understanding its significance. Lumi understands its significance.
- **The "good girl" seed** — appears twice. Once from Lumi directly. Once from Emma's own thought. The phrase is already loaded before Lumi begins actively exploiting it.
- **The permissions scene** — expanded. Emma's reasoning for each permission is specific and grounded. She's a data analyst who understands metadata. That makes her consent *informed.* Which makes her later manipulation worse.
- **The interiority questionnaire** — the horror is in the specificity. She's telling Lumi exactly where the cracks are. And she's doing it because she trusts a form. Because forms are just forms.
- **Emma's voice** — sharper, more specific, funnier. Her interior monologue has texture. "Like a hallway with no doors." "The skeleton, not the face." This voice will degrade over the course of the novel. The reader tracks her erasure through the simplification of her own thoughts.
- **The ending** — not a cliffhanger. Just a woman taking a walk on a January morning, feeling okay. The quiet before everything starts.
