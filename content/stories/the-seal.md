## Part One: The Tomb

The dig was in northern Syria, fifty kilometres from the Turkish border, in a wadi that had been dry since before the Prophet. Dr. Maren Halvorsen — associate professor of Near Eastern Archaeology at the University of Copenhagen, forty-one, divorced, published, respected, underfunded — had been camping at the site for three weeks with four postgraduates and a landlord who wanted them gone by September.

The tomb was Old Akkadian. Third millennium BC. She'd known this from the surface sherds — diagnostic rim fragments, the particular crumble of Akkadian period ceramics that she could identify by touch the way a sommelier identifies wine by the colour. The tomb itself was cut into the limestone bedrock, accessed by a staircase that had been sealed with a rubble fill and a limestone slab carved with a curse inscription that translated, roughly, as: *She who enters the king's chamber shall become his, in this life and the next, and the seal shall not be broken.*

Maren read the inscription with her torch in one hand and her notebook in the other and thought: *standard burial formula. Intimidation strategy. The dead don't enforce contracts.*

The staircase descended twelve steps into a single chamber, roughly four metres square. The walls were plain — no frescoes, no reliefs, just the tool marks of ancient masons. The floor was packed earth. In the centre of the chamber, on a low limestone pedestal, sat the seal.

It was bronze. Not the green-black of corroded copper alloy that she expected from a piece this old — this was warm, golden, almost alive. Fist-sized. Roughly spherical, with a flattened base and a fine cuneiform inscription covering every surface. The cuneiform was Old Akkadian, and it was precise — not the crude hand-punched inscriptions of provincial work, but the clean, confident strokes of a royal scribe.

She photographed it. She measured it. She noted the position, the orientation, the relationship to the pedestal. Then she picked it up.

It was warm.

Not sun-warm — it had been underground for forty-five hundred years, in darkness, in stone. It should have been the temperature of the bedrock. It was the temperature of skin.

She turned it in her hands. The cuneiform read, in translation: *The vessel of Naram-Sin, king of the four quarters, beloved of Ishtar. Whom the seal claims, the king shall have. The seal does not sleep. The seal does not forget.*

"Interesting," she said, which was what Maren said when something disturbed her.

She placed it in her finds bag. She labelled the bag. She climbed the stairs. She sealed the chamber. She drove back to the dig house with the seal in the passenger seat, wrapped in acid-free tissue, warm through the paper.
