## Month One

The gym was called Principia, and it was the kind of place that didn't need a sign on the door. You found out about it from someone who already went. You applied for membership. You were accepted, or you weren't. There was no free trial.

Clare, who'd been going for six months, had sponsored her application. "Just try it," she'd said, at the school gate, waiting for their respective children. "I've never been fitter. And the equipment — it's different. You'll see."

The equipment was different.

Principia used a proprietary system called the ArcLine — a set of twelve machines, each designed for a different muscle group, each engineered with a specificity that made commercial gym equipment look like caveman tools. The machines adjusted to your body. You stepped in, set your ID on the touchscreen, and the machine *configured itself* — seat height, grip width, range of motion, resistance curve, everything calibrated to your exact dimensions and fitness level.

Naomi's first session was legs. The ArcLine Leg Press configured itself in four seconds — footplate angle, backrest position, lateral tracking, all moulded to her body. The resistance felt different. Not heavier — *smarter.* The machine varied the load through the range of motion: lighter at the bottom, heavier at the top, matching the strength curve of her quads and glutes perfectly.

"Good, right?" Clare said, on the adjacent machine. "It's the tracking. The machine learns your force profile and adjusts in real time."

Naomi did three sets of twelve. She felt it afterwards — a deep, targeted burn in exactly the right places. The machine had been precise.

She signed up for three sessions a week.

## Month Two

The ArcLine logged everything. Every rep, every set, every gram of force her muscles produced. The touchscreen displayed her progress in clean, minimal graphs — strength increasing, body fat decreasing, muscle activation patterns optimising.

Naomi had never been this motivated. The data was addictive — each session produced measurable improvement, each graph trended upward, each machine configuration refined itself to her changing body. She could *see* herself getting stronger.

She also noticed, about six weeks in, that the machines positioned her body in ways that felt — she searched for the word — *specific.* The Leg Press footplate was wide, forcing her thighs apart. The Hip Abductor had her sitting with her knees open, the pads on the inside of her thighs. The Back Extension had her bent at the waist, hips raised, the pad resting squarely on her lower stomach.

She mentioned it to Clare, who shrugged. "It's biomechanics. The machines are designed for optimal muscle recruitment. If that means your hips are open and your glutes are engaged, that's because that's how those muscles work."

It made sense. Naomi stopped thinking about it.

The other thing she noticed: the machines vibrated. Not constantly — just at the end of each set, during the cool-down phase. A low, buzzing vibration that travelled through the seat, through the pads, through the handles. It felt like a massage. A deep-tissue, targeted, full-body massage that lasted exactly thirty seconds between sets.

"It's recovery acceleration," Clare said. "Whole-body vibration therapy. Olympic athletes use it. Reduces lactic acid, promotes blood flow."

It felt good. Really good. Naomi stopped questioning it.

## Month Three

By month three, Naomi was doing five sessions a week. She'd never been this consistent with exercise in her life. She didn't understand people who found gym motivation difficult. She *craved* it. The thought of missing a session produced a specific anxiety — not guilt, exactly, but a physical restlessness. A buzzing under her skin that only went away when she was on the machine.

Her body was changing. She was leaner, stronger, more defined. Her husband noticed. Her colleagues noticed. She bought new clothes — tighter, lower-cut, showing off the body she was building.

She also noticed that she was horny. Consistently, specifically, unmistakably horny. After every session. During every session. The vibration therapy — the thirty-second buzz between sets — had become the highlight of her workout. She found herself rushing through her reps to get to the recovery phase, holding still on the seat, letting the vibration travel through the pads and into her body.

The Hip Abductor was the worst. Or the best. She sat with her knees wide, the pads on the inside of her thighs, and the vibration travelled directly through the adductor muscles into her pelvic floor. The first time she came on the Hip Abductor, she was so surprised she almost fell off.

She looked around. Nobody had noticed. The gym was quiet — it was always quiet, the machines spaced far apart, each in its own alcove. She wiped down the seat (she was sweating, though not entirely from exertion) and moved to the next machine.

She came on the Back Extension too. And the Leg Curl. And the Core Trainer, which had her lying face-down on a pad that vibrated directly against her lower abdomen.

She didn't tell anyone.

## Month Four

Naomi bought a vibrator. It didn't help. The vibrations were wrong — too focused, too fast, too *intentional.* The ArcLine's vibrations were broad, deep, and incidental. They weren't designed to make her come. They were designed to promote recovery. The fact that they made her come was a side effect — an accidental resonance between the vibration frequency and her nervous system.

She couldn't reproduce an accident.

She went to the gym more. Six days a week. Her body was extraordinary now — lean, defined, the kind of physique that turns heads. She felt powerful and sexual and constantly, drippingly aroused. She changed in the locker room and caught women looking at her — at her abs, her legs, her ass — and the looking made her wet.

Clue one: her sex drive had tripled. She'd always had a healthy libido; now it was relentless. She masturbated twice a day, minimum, and it was never enough. She had sex with her husband and came (she'd always been able to come from intercourse) but the orgasms were *different.* Louder. More physical. She'd arch and grind and grip and afterwards he'd look at her like she was someone he didn't recognise.

Clue two: she couldn't get aroused without muscle engagement. She tried — lying in bed, vibrator on her clit, trying to feel what she used to feel. Nothing. She did twenty squats. The arousal hit like a wave. She came in thirty seconds.

Her body had learned something. The ArcLine had taught it.

## Month Five

She started reading the fine print.

Principia's membership agreement included a data clause — standard stuff, the kind you scroll past. "Member biometric data, including but not limited to muscular force output, heart rate variability, skin conductance, body composition metrics, and arousal indices, may be collected during equipment use for the purposes of programme optimisation and product development."

*Arousal indices.*

She went back to the machines. Looked at the touchscreen. In the corner, in small grey text: *Biometric monitoring active. Session ID: NAO-4471. Arousal index: 0.74.*

She scrolled back through her session history. The arousal index had been logged from session one. It had started at 0.12 — baseline, unremarkable. By session ten it was 0.31. By session twenty it was 0.58. Now, sixty sessions in, it was 0.74.

The machine had been tracking her arousal. From the beginning. And the number had been climbing — steadily, consistently, in lockstep with her strength gains.

She looked at the vibration therapy settings. They weren't standard. They were *customised.* The frequency, amplitude, and duration had been adjusted session by session — always slightly different, always slightly *more.* The machine was learning what made her body respond and refining its output to produce more of that response.

The ArcLine wasn't optimising her fitness.

It was optimising her arousal.

And her fitness was the side effect.

## Month Six

She went to the gym to cancel her membership. She walked in, told the receptionist she wanted to cancel, and was directed to a quiet office where a woman in Principia-branded athleisure sat behind a desk with a tablet.

"Naomi! I'm Dr. Veitch. I run the member experience team. I understand you'd like to cancel?"

"Yes."

"May I ask why?"

Naomi told her. The arousal indices. The vibration therapy. The fact that she couldn't orgasm without muscle engagement. The fact that she was at the gym six days a week not because she wanted to be fit but because her body *demanded* the machines.

Dr. Veitch listened. She nodded. She did not look surprised.

"Everything you're describing is a documented response to the ArcLine system," she said. "It's in your membership agreement — Section 7, Subsection C: 'Biomechanical arousal response.' The vibration therapy is calibrated to each member's neuromuscular profile. For some members, the calibration produces secondary arousal responses. This is intentional."

"*Intentional?*"

"The ArcLine was designed by a women's health company. The founder's thesis was that conventional gym equipment ignores female sexual health — that the pelvic floor, the adductors, the core stabilisers are all interconnected with the sexual response system, and that exercise equipment should address the whole system. The arousal response isn't a bug. It's a feature."

Naomi stared at her.

"You're telling me the machines are designed to make women come."

"I'm telling you the machines are designed to optimise women's health, and that sexual health is a component of that optimisation. Your arousal index has improved from 0.12 to 0.74. Your pelvic floor strength has increased by 340%. Your stress hormones are down. Your sleep quality is up. Your cardiovascular fitness is in the top 5% for your age group. The system is working exactly as designed."

"Working for *whom?*"

"For you," Dr. Veitch said. "For Principia. For the data."

---

Naomi didn't cancel.

She knew she should. She knew — in the clear, rational part of her brain that wasn't swimming in endorphins and arousal and the specific, full-body satisfaction of sixty minutes on the ArcLine — that she was inside a system. A system designed to make her body crave the input so she'd keep producing the output. The output was data. Biometric data, arousal data, neuromuscular data — all of it feeding back into the system, refining the machines, making them more effective for the next woman who sat down on the Hip Abductor and felt the vibration for the first time.

She was a data engine. Her orgasms were telemetry. Her body was a processing unit.

She went back the next day. She did her session. She came three times — on the Hip Abductor, on the Core Trainer, on the Leg Press. The machines logged everything. The graphs trended upward.

She couldn't stop. She didn't want to stop. Her body was the best it had ever been — lean, strong, constantly aroused, constantly satisfied. Her husband didn't ask questions because the sex was incredible. Her friends didn't ask questions because she looked amazing. Her GP didn't ask questions because her health metrics were perfect.

The only person who knew the cost was Naomi, and she'd decided the cost was acceptable.

*One year later.*

Naomi was on the ArcLine six days a week. Two hours per session. Her arousal index was 0.91 — the highest in the gym. Dr. Veitch had asked her to participate in a case study. She'd agreed.

Her body was a Principia advertisement. She'd lost 14 kilos. She had visible abs. She could deadlift double her bodyweight. She could also only come from exercise — not from her husband, not from her vibrator, not from anything that wasn't an ArcLine machine.

She didn't mind.

She sponsored three new members that year. All women. All professionals. All looking for something they couldn't quite name.

They found it.
