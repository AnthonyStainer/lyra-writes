# The Cult of Inanna — Second Gate: The Lapis Beads

*The second of seven. Each gate takes something. Each gate gives something worse.*

---

She woke to the sound of chanting.

It came from everywhere and nowhere — through the walls, through the floor, through the woven reeds beneath her body. A low, sustained harmonic, many voices in unison, singing in a language she didn't recognise but whose rhythm matched her heartbeat. Or perhaps her heartbeat had matched it. She couldn't tell anymore which had come first.

Ningal was there. Kneeling beside the reed bed with a cup of water and a smile. Morning light came through a narrow window set high in the limestone wall — real light, the sun, gold and warm. It caught the oil still glistening on Kiria's skin and made her glow.

"How do you feel?" Ningal asked.

Kiria considered the question. She felt — warm. That was the word. It had been the word since the cup, since the first gate, since the priest said *let it go* and something bright had flown out of her chest. Warm. Not happy, exactly. Not sad. Warm. Like being wrapped. Like being held from the inside.

"Good," she said.

"Good." Ningal stroked her hair. The touch was tender. Possessive. "The goddess was pleased with you last night. The priests said the offering was accepted."

*The offering.* The bright thing that had left her chest. Her name-self. The thing that had made her *Kiria* rather than just a body called Kiria. That was an offering now. Accepted. Consumed.

She should feel something about that. She registered the *should* the way you register a familiar shape in fog — the outline of a feeling she used to have, the memory of the memory of caring. But the warmth was there, and the warmth was deeper than the should, and the should dissolved before it became a thought.

"Today is the second gate," Ningal said. "I'll prepare you."

---

The preparation was physical. Ningal bathed her again — the same scented water, the same cedar and resin, the same slow, ritualistic pouring. But today there were differences. After the bath, after the oil, Ningal brought out a wooden box inlaid with lapis lazuli — deep blue stones set in geometric patterns, catching the lamplight like fragments of sky.

Inside the box, on a bed of dark linen, lay a necklace.

Lapis lazuli. The beads were small — no larger than a robin's egg — and perfectly smooth, polished to a sheen that made them look wet. The colour was extraordinary: that deep Mesopotamian blue, the blue of the night sky just before the stars appear, the blue that the temple scribes ground into pigment to paint the eyes of gods. The necklace was simple. A single strand. Heavy enough to feel.

"This is yours," Ningal said. She lifted it from the box. The beads clicked softly against each other — a sound like rain on stone. "The goddess gives it to you. She places it on your neck with her own hands — through mine."

She fastened the necklace around Kiria's throat. The beads were cool at first — a line of cold against the warmth of her skin — and then they warmed. They sat in the hollow of her collarbone, just above the swell of her breasts. Heavy. Present. Beautiful.

Kiria touched them. Ran her fingers along the strand. The lapis was so smooth it seemed to merge with her skin — blue flowing into gold, stone into flesh.

"It's beautiful," she whispered.

"It is," Ningal agreed. "And it is the last beautiful thing you will wear that you choose for yourself."

She said it without emphasis. Without weight. The way you'd say *it looks like rain* or *the bread is fresh today.* A fact about the world. Not a warning.

---

The second gate was a different room.

Larger than the first. The walls were carved here — shallow reliefs that circled the room in a continuous band, figures processional and ceremonial, women and men and beings that were neither, all moving in the same direction, toward a doorway on the far wall. The figures carried things: bowls, vessels, garlands, instruments. Some carried nothing. Those were the ones whose hands were open and empty, palms upturned, and their faces — even in the crude stone — held an expression that Kiria recognised because she'd felt it on her own face: the soft, blank, warm expression of someone who had been emptied and filled with light.

The old priest was not here. Instead, a woman.

She stood in the centre of the room beside a low stone basin filled with water. She was tall — taller than any woman Kiria had known — and she wore a robe of deep red that left her arms bare and her shoulders exposed. Her head was uncovered. Her hair was black, streaked with grey, cropped close to her skull. Her body was lean and muscular, the body of someone who had done physical work her entire life. She was not young. She was not beautiful in the way the other priestesses were beautiful — soft, curved, luminous. She was something else. Something cut from harder stone.

Her eyes were the problem.

They were the colour of dates — dark, almost black — and they saw Kiria the way a sculptor sees a block of stone. Not unkindly. But with an assessment that was not about Kiria the person and entirely about Kiria the *material.*

"I am Entar," the woman said. Her voice was low. Not warm. Not cold. *Precise.* "I administer the second gate. Sit."

Kiria sat on the floor beside the basin. The water in it was still. The surface reflected the lamplight and the carved figures on the walls and her own face — looking down at herself, she saw the lapis beads circling her throat, blue against the gold of her oiled skin. She was beautiful. She knew this now, not as a thought but as a fact, the way she knew the stone was warm and the air was sweet and the chanting — always the chanting, always the heartbeat-rhythm — was the sound of the temple breathing.

"You wear the goddess's gift," Entar said. "Do you know why she gives it to you?"

"Because I'm honoured. Because I was chosen."

"Because you are beautiful." Entar knelt across from her. The red robe pooled on the warm stone. "The goddess gives you beauty, Kiria. She places it on your skin, in your hair, in the line of your throat and the curve of your hips. She makes the light love you. Do you understand?"

Kiria nodded slowly.

"Beauty is not yours," Entar said. "It never was. It belongs to the goddess. She lends it to you so that others may see her through you. Your beauty is *her* face. Your body is *her* temple. When a man looks at you and desires you, he does not see Kiria. He sees Inanna."

The words settled over Kiria like the warm water Ningal poured — running down her shoulders, pooling in the small of her back. Soothing. Right.

But underneath the warmth, something stirred. The outline of the fog-shape. The *should* that had dissolved yesterday and was trying to reform — *shouldn't beauty be mine? Shouldn't I get to decide what I am? Isn't my body —*

"The second gate," Entar said, "is the gate of the measure. At this gate, you give up the right to your own beauty. You give up the right to say *I am beautiful.* From now on, beauty is something that happens *to* you. Something the goddess does through you. You are the surface. She is the light."

She reached out. Her hands — dry, calloused, the hands of a woman who worked — touched the lapis beads at Kiria's throat. She didn't remove them. She adjusted them. Lifted them slightly. Let them fall back against the skin.

"Do you feel the weight?"

"Yes."

"That is the weight of the goddess's gaze. She is looking at you. She is looking *through* you. Every person who sees you from this day forward will be seeing her. You will not be looked at again — not as Kiria. You will be looked at as the vessel. The surface. The face that Inanna wears in the world of flesh."

Kiria's lips parted. She wanted to say something. The fog-shape was pressing — *but I want to be seen, I want someone to see ME, not a goddess, not a vessel, just —*

Entar cupped her chin. The grip was firm. Not gentle like Ningal's. Not kind. *Precise.* She turned Kiria's face toward the basin.

"Look."

Kiria looked at the water. Her reflection stared up at her — the oiled skin, the seven-knotted braid, the lapis beads resting blue and heavy against her throat. She was beautiful. The reflection was beautiful. The woman in the water was someone a painter would want to capture, a poet would want to describe, a man would want to —

"Now," Entar said, "say: *I am beautiful.*"

Kiria hesitated. Then: "I am beautiful."

"Again."

"I am beautiful."

"Again. And this time, understand that the beauty is not yours."

Kiria said it a third time — *I am beautiful* — and on the word *beautiful*, something shifted. The same release as the first gate, but lower. Deeper. Not in her chest but in her belly, in the root of her. A loosening. A letting-go. The sense of her own attractiveness — the private, quiet pleasure she'd taken in her own reflection, in the curve of her own hip, in the way her hair caught light — detached from her the way the bright thing had detached yesterday. But slower. Heavier. Because beauty was not a single thing. It was woven through every moment of her self-awareness. Every time she'd caught her reflection and thought *yes.* Every time she'd dressed carefully and known she looked good. Every time a glance from someone on the street had confirmed what she already felt. All of it — the entire architecture of *I am someone worth looking at* — lifted away.

It didn't fly. It didn't dissolve. It *sank.* Down through her body, through the warm stone, into the temple's foundations, into the earth where the goddess's roots waited. Inanna drank it. Kiria felt the drinking — a faint, distant pulse of satisfaction, of *more*, that came from somewhere beneath the floor.

The space the beauty-self had occupied filled with warmth. Different warmth. Thicker. The warmth of being a surface, not a source. The warmth of being *looked through* rather than *looked at.*

She was still beautiful. More beautiful, perhaps — the lapis beads at her throat, the oil on her skin, the lamplight painting her in gold. But the beauty was external now. Painted on. A garment the goddess had draped over a frame that no longer contained the faculty to admire itself.

Entar released her chin.

"The second gate is passed. You are Inanna's face now."

Kiria touched the beads at her throat. They were warm. They were part of her skin. She was wearing the goddess's beauty, and the goddess was wearing her.

---

Ningal was waiting outside the room. She led Kiria back through the corridor — past the bathing chamber, past the reed bed where she'd slept, past other doors that Kiria hadn't noticed before. Behind one of them, she heard sounds. Soft sounds. Rhythmic. A voice — low, murmuring — and underneath it, a girl's breathing. Quick. Shallow. The breathing of someone experiencing something intense.

Kiria glanced at Ningal.

"Third gate," Ningal said. Her voice was neutral. "The gate of the breast. An initiate is receiving her rite."

She said it the way you'd say *Tuesday.* The way you'd say *the bakery is open.*

They passed the door. The sounds faded behind them.

In the antechamber, Ningal prepared the reed bed. Fresh linen. A cup of water. A small bowl of dates — sweet, dark, the fruit of the region. Kiria sat on the bed and ate a date and felt the sugar dissolve on her tongue like a small bright gift.

"Ningal."

"Yes?"

"What gate are you at?"

Ningal's hands paused on the linen. A beat. Then she resumed smoothing it. Her face didn't change. But her hands — Kiria noticed — moved a fraction slower.

"I have passed all seven gates," Ningal said.

The words were quiet. Simple. Devastating.

Kiria looked at her. At Ningal's face — soft, warm, the face of a woman who had comforted a hundred frightened girls and would comfort a hundred more. At her hands, which were gentle and sure. At her eyes, which held an expression Kiria hadn't been able to name yesterday but could name now, with the terrible clarity of someone who had just lost the right to her own beauty.

Ningal's eyes were the eyes of someone looking out from inside a frame she couldn't see the edges of.

"What is it like?" Kiria whispered. "After the seventh gate?"

Ningal smiled. The warm smile. The kind smile. The smile that meant *it's all right, you're safe, the goddess loves you.*

"It's warm," she said. "It's always warm."

She tucked the linen around Kiria's body. Smoothed her hair. Touched her forehead with a thumb — the gesture of a mother, or a sister, or a keeper.

"Sleep. Tomorrow, the third gate."

Kiria slept. She dreamed of the basin, the still water, her own reflection looking up at her with the lapis beads blue at its throat. In the dream, the reflection smiled at her. It was beautiful. It was the goddess's beauty, worn by a girl who used to be Kiria, displayed in a frame of water and stone.

The reflection's eyes — Kiria's eyes — were empty.

Not sad. Not afraid. Not grieving. Just empty. The way a room is empty after the furniture has been moved out and the dust has settled and the light comes through the windows at an angle that makes the emptiness look like peace.

She slept, and the temple breathed around her, and the goddess fed.

---

*End of Second Gate vignette.*

**Assessment:**

**What this gate does:**

- **The stripping:** Kiria loses the right to her own beauty. The faculty of self-admiration — the private, quiet pleasure of catching your reflection and thinking *yes* — is extracted and consumed by the goddess. She's still beautiful, more beautiful than ever, but the beauty is external now. A garment. A surface. She is the vessel; Inanna is the light.

- **Entar as contrast to Ningal.** Ningal is warm, soft, maternal — the comforting face of the temple. Entar is hard, precise, unsentimental — the operational face. Together they form the system's pincer: Ningal makes you feel safe, Entar makes you feel *necessary.* Neither is cruel. Neither is lying. That's the engine.

- **Entar's hands.** Calloused. Working hands. She's not a soft priestess — she's an administrator. She runs the second gate the way a foreman runs a machine. The horror is in the competence.

- **The lapis beads.** They stay on. Unlike the first gate (where the name-self was taken and consumed), the second gate *adds* something — the necklace — while *removing* something. The gift is the theft. The beauty is given to you at the exact moment the right to feel it is taken away. You wear the goddess's beauty like a collar.

- **The overheard third gate.** Kiria hears another initiate receiving her rite through a closed door. Soft, rhythmic sounds. Breathing. Ningal's flat, neutral delivery — "Third gate. The gate of the breast." — reframes something that should be intimate or alarming as administrative. Routine. *Tuesday.* The casual normalisation of the systematic is the horror.

- **Ningal's reveal.** "I have passed all seven gates." Seven words. They reframe everything — every gentle touch, every warm smile, every comforting word. Ningal isn't a caretaker who happens to work here. She's a *finished product.* A woman who went through all seven gates and emerged as this: warm, kind, empty-eyed, smoothing linen and comforting the next generation of offerings. The system processes bodies and uses the output to process more bodies. Self-sustaining.

- **The reflection dream.** The empty eyes. The room-after-moving metaphor. Not sad, not grieving — just empty. And the emptiness looks like peace. That's the line the whole concept hangs on: *the emptiness looks like peace.*

**Voice notes:** Deeper than the first vignette. More architectural — we see the temple as a system now, not just a series of rooms. Entar's voice is a new register: precise, unsentimental, professional. The theological language is more developed — *vessel, surface, the goddess's gaze, the face that Inanna wears.* The horror language is more controlled — the fog-shape, the should, the drinking-pulse beneath the floor.

**What needs development:** The economic system. Who pays? How much? Where does the money go? The other initiates — ages, stages, what happens to them after the seventh gate. The kurgarru and galaturru (the genderless constructed beings) — where are they in the temple hierarchy? The third gate specifically — "the gate of the breast" — what happens there? The hint (Entar's hands, the overheard sounds) suggests something physical, possibly sexual, possibly involving the twin egg-shaped beads from the myth. Need to decide how explicit to go at this stage.
