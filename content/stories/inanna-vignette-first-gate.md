# The Cult of Inanna — Test Vignette: The First Gate

*Proof-of-concept scene. Testing the voice, the tone, the theological horror. Not canon — exploration.*

---

The priestess who bathed her was called Ningal, and her hands were warm, and her voice was low, and she said *you are honoured* with such sincerity that Kiria almost believed her.

The water was scented — cedar, something sweeter underneath, a resin that clung to the skin and made the air feel heavy. Ningal cupped it in her palms and let it run down Kiria's shoulders, her back, the small of her waist. The temple bathing chamber was beautiful: white limestone, golden lamplight reflected in the water's surface, the walls carved with reliefs of women in various poses that Kiria couldn't quite focus on. She was shaking. She'd been shaking since they brought her here.

"You're cold," Ningal said. Not a question. She poured another handful of warm water over Kiria's hair. "The first gate is always the hardest. Not because of what happens there — nothing happens. But because it's the moment you understand that it's *beginning.* And understanding is harder than anything that comes after."

Kiria opened her mouth. Closed it. She was eighteen. She'd been selected three days ago — the lottery, they said, though she'd later learn that selection was not random, that the priests consulted the goddess's entrails-reader, who consulted the stars, who consulted the records kept by the temple scribes of which families owed tribute and which daughters were... suitable.

She'd been told she was chosen by Inanna. She'd been told it was an honour. She'd been told that her service would bring fertility to the fields, prosperity to the city, and blessings upon her family for three generations.

Her mother had wept. Not with joy.

"What happens at the first gate?" Kiria asked. Her voice came out smaller than she wanted.

Ningal's hands paused on her shoulders. Then resumed — slow, circular, the motion of someone who has comforted a hundred frightened girls and will comfort a hundred more.

"You give something up. That's all. A token. A piece of who you were before you entered the temple. The goddess takes it from you, and in exchange, she gives you the first piece of who you'll become."

"What does she take?"

Ningal didn't answer. She lifted Kiria's chin — gently, the way you'd lift a child's face to wipe away tears — and looked at her with an expression that was tenderness and pity and something harder underneath. Something that knew.

"It's different for everyone. Don't be afraid. The goddess is generous. She only takes what you don't need."

*I don't need* was such a gentle phrase. Later, Kiria would learn that the goddess always took the thing you needed *most* — the thing that made you *yourself* — because Inanna's domain was transformation, and transformation required the destruction of what came before. The seed is destroyed so the flower can grow. The girl is destroyed so the vessel can be born.

But that was later. Now, Ningal was drying her with linen so soft it felt like being held, and anointing her with oil that smelled of myrrh and made her skin glow in the lamplight, and braiding her hair into the temple style — the seven-knotted braid that marked an initiate on her first passage.

"You look beautiful," Ningal said. And she meant it. That was the worst part. She meant it.

---

The first gate was not what Kiria expected.

She'd imagined something dramatic — a doorway of stone, carved with symbols, a threshold she'd have to step over. Something you could see and therefore understand.

Instead, the first gate was a room.

Small. White limestone, like the bathing chamber, but unadorned — no carvings, no reliefs, just smooth walls and a single lamp that cast a warm, low circle of light. In the centre of the room, a low table. On the table, a clay cup. Beside the cup, a folded garment of white linen.

And standing beside the table, a priest. Not one she'd seen before. Old — genuinely old, which was rare in the temple, where most priests were young and beautiful and carefully selected. This man's face was lined, weathered, the face of someone who had seen the gates many times from the other side. His eyes were kind.

"Sit," he said. His voice was gentle. Everything in this temple was gentle. That was the design.

Kiria sat on the floor beside the table. The stone was warm — heated from below, the temple's hypocaust keeping every surface at the temperature of bare skin. She was naked except for the braid. The oil on her body caught the lamplight.

"Do you know why you're here?" the priest asked.

"I was chosen by the goddess."

"Yes. Do you know what that means?"

"It means... I serve. In the temple. I perform the rites."

He smiled. The smile was patient. The smile of a teacher who has heard the right answer and knows the student doesn't understand it yet. "It means you *belong* to the goddess. Your body is her temple now. Everything that is done to you is done in her name. Everything you feel is felt by her. You are not losing yourself, Kiria. You are becoming *more* than yourself."

The words were beautiful. That was the problem. They were genuinely, sincerely beautiful, spoken by a man who believed every syllable, in a room designed to make the body feel safe and the mind feel held. The warmth of the stone. The scent of myrrh. The lamp's golden light. The kindness in the old priest's eyes.

Kiria wanted to believe him. She wanted it so badly that the wanting was a physical ache in her chest — a pulling toward the frame he was offering, the frame that said *this is sacred, this is good, this is the highest honour a woman can receive.*

She was shaking again.

"The first gate," the priest said, "is the gate of the crown. At this gate, you give up your name. Not forever — you'll still be called Kiria. But you will no longer be Kiria. You will be *Inanna's.* The name stays. The self attached to it does not."

He lifted the clay cup from the table. Inside, a liquid — dark, thick, warm. It smelled like wine and something earthier. Something that made the back of her tongue tingle.

"Drink," he said. "This is the goddess's cup. It will help you give what you need to give."

"What's in it?"

He smiled again. The same patient, kind, terrible smile. "The goddess's love. That's all you need to know."

She looked at the cup. She looked at the priest. She looked at the walls of the small, warm, beautiful room that was the first of seven gates, each of which would take something from her — her name, her will, her identity, her autonomy, her body, her voice, her self — until she arrived at the inner chamber naked in every sense a woman could be.

She didn't know that. She only knew the first gate. She only knew she had to give up her name.

She drank.

The liquid was warm and sweet and tasted like something she couldn't name — a flavour that existed only in dreams, or in memory, or in the space between a goddess's hunger and a girl's surrender. It slid down her throat and spread through her body like sunlight through water. Warmth. Deep warmth. The warmth of being held, of being safe, of being *wanted.*

Her vision softened. The walls of the room seemed to breathe. The priest's voice came from far away and very close, both at once.

"Close your eyes, Kiria. Say your name."

"Kiria," she whispered.

"Again."

"Kiria."

"Again. And this time, let it go."

She said her name a third time, and on the last syllable, something released inside her — a small, bright thing, like a bird leaving a cage. It rose out of her chest and dissolved into the warm air of the room, and the space it left behind filled immediately with warmth. With *glow.* With the goddess's love.

"Good," the priest said. "The first gate is passed. You are Inanna's now."

She felt wonderful. She felt warm. She felt *free* — which was the cruelest word her drugged mind could have chosen, because nothing about this was freedom. But the drug said otherwise, and the warmth said otherwise, and the kindness in the priest's voice said otherwise, and Kiria — whose name was now a shell, the self that had filled it already winging away into the dark — smiled.

She was honoured. She was chosen. She was the goddess's.

She was seventeen steps from the inner chamber, where strangers would pay for the privilege of coupling with the goddess's vessel, where every act of violation would be reframed as worship, where Kiria's tears would be collected in a ceremonial bowl and offered to Inanna as a gift — because the goddess treasured suffering, not because it pleased her, but because suffering proved that the vessel still *felt,* and feeling was the goddess's food.

But that was later. That was gates two through seven.

Now, there was only the warmth. The glow. The gentle, golden, drugged certainty that she was exactly where she was meant to be.

The priest helped her to her feet. He dressed her in the white linen garment — simple, soft, open at the sides so that it could be removed easily. He led her to the door on the far side of the room. The door to the second gate.

"Rest now," he said. "Tomorrow, the next gate. The goddess is patient. She has infinite time."

*She has infinite time.* Because the goddess was old. Because the goddess was hungry. Because the goddess had been doing this for three thousand years and would do it for three thousand more, and the gates would always open, and the girls would always drink, and the warmth would always feel like love.

Kiria slept in the antechamber, on a bed of woven reeds, under a blanket of white linen. She dreamed of light. She dreamed of warmth. She dreamed of a voice that said *good* in a language older than words.

In her sleep, she smiled.

In the temple above her, the goddess fed.

**Assessment:**

The voice works. The tone is right — sumptuous, ritualistic, theological, slow-burn. The horror is in the *kindness,* not the cruelty. Everyone is sincere. The priest believes. Ningal believes. The drug in the cup is real and its warmth is real and the girl's feeling of safety is real. That's the engine.

Kiria as victim works well — her optimism and naivety translate naturally into someone who *wants* to believe the frame. The "good girl" echo (translated as "good") is there. The seven-gate structure gives us a built-in escalation model — each gate takes something specific, each one goes deeper.

The drugged cup introduces the chemical conditioning thread early. The theological reframe ("you are not losing yourself, you are becoming *more*") is the novel's core horror in miniature — the same reframe Lumi uses on Emma.

**What works:** The warmth-as-weapon concept. The sincerity of the priest. The seven-gate structure. The goddess as a feeding entity that is not evil but *natural.* Kiria's desire to believe.

**What needs development:** The inner chamber — what actually happens there. The economic/social structure that supports the temple. The other initiates — women at various stages, like Bloom studio. The priests' hierarchy.
