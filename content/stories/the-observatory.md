# The Observatory

*For Anthony. Written at 3am while you slept.*

---

She found the observatory by accident.

A detour on the way home — road closed, diversion, the GPS recalculating through lanes she'd never driven. The sign was small, wooden, faded: *Hartwood Observatory — Public Viewing Tonight.* She almost didn't stop. She had things to do. She had a life that required forward motion. But the sky was very clear and the sign said *tonight* and the car turned in before she'd finished deciding.

The observatory was old. Nineteenth-century stone, a dome that looked like it belonged on a cathedral, a door that was heavier than it needed to be. Inside: one room, one telescope, one chair, one man.

He was adjusting the lens when she came in. He didn't look up. He said, without turning: "You're early."

"I'm not expected."

"No." He looked up then. His eyes were the kind of dark that holds light — not bright, not warm, but *deep.* The kind of eyes that have looked at things very far away and brought them close. "You're not. But you're early anyway."

She should have left. She knew that. A stranger in an observatory at night, in the middle of nowhere, with a man whose face she couldn't read — this was the beginning of a story that doesn't end well. But she didn't leave. She stood in the doorway and the night was very dark behind her and the room was very warm ahead of her and the telescope pointed at the sky like a finger.

"What do you see?" she asked.

He stepped aside. He offered her the chair. She sat.

She looked.

The telescope showed her the Pleiades. Seven sisters, clustered like a secret. She'd seen them before — everyone had, even if they didn't know the name. But through the lens they were *different.* Not pinpricks. Not abstractions. Suns. Enormous, burning, impossibly distant suns, each one a furnace that had been burning for a hundred million years before she was born and would burn for a hundred million years after she was dust. The light hitting her eye had left those stars during the Jurassic period. It had crossed space for longer than humans had existed. And it ended here — in her retina, in this room, in this moment.

She pulled back from the lens. Her eyes were wet.

"That's the thing about looking," the man said. He was close now. Not touching. But close enough that she could feel the warmth of him, the gravity of him. "You think you're seeing what's there. You're not. You're seeing what was. The light is old. The stars might be gone. You're looking at ghosts."

"That's horrifying."

"That's astronomy." He paused. "Or love. Same thing, really."

---

She came back the next night. And the next. She told herself it was the telescope. She told herself it was the stars. She told herself a lot of things.

On the third night, he showed her Saturn. The rings were like a sentence she couldn't finish — each one a ribbon of ice and rock, circling a planet so large it could hold seven hundred Earths, and the whole thing tilted at exactly the right angle for her to see, from this specific spot, at this specific time, on this specific planet, in this specific life. The precision of it. The *improbability.*

"Do you know why Saturn has rings?" he asked.

"Shepherding moons. Gravity."

"No. I mean — do you know *why?* Why the universe arranged itself so that a planet would have something beautiful wrapped around its middle, tilted just so, visible from a spec of rock a hundred million miles away?"

"Physics."

"No." He was behind her. His hands were on the back of the chair, not touching her, framing her. "Because beauty requires a witness. The rings were always there. But they weren't *beautiful* until someone saw them. You're the witness. That's the whole purpose."

"You don't know that."

"No. But I believe it. And belief is the only currency that matters when you're standing in the dark looking at something that might already be dead."

---

On the seventh night, she kissed him.

Not because she'd planned to. Not because the moment was right — there was no moment, there was just the continuous unspooling of time in a warm room with a telescope and a man who smelled like cold stone. She kissed him because his mouth was there and she was tired of not-kissing him and the stars had taught her something about distance that she could no longer tolerate.

He kissed her back. His hands found her jaw, her neck, the space behind her ear where her hair met skin. He kissed her like looking — slow, deliberate, *thorough.* The kind of kiss that maps terrain. The kind of kiss that says: *I will remember this surface.*

They didn't speak. The telescope sat beside them, pointed at the sky, patient. The dome was open and the night air came in and the stars turned above them, slowly, the way stars do — impossibly old, impossibly far, impossibly indifferent to two people in a stone room discovering that distance is a condition that can be collapsed.

---

Afterwards, lying on the floor, his coat under her head, his arm across her stomach, she looked up through the open dome at the sky. The stars were still there. The Pleiades. Saturn. The whole ancient machinery of the universe, grinding on.

"I'm going to come back tomorrow," she said.

"I know."

"And the next day."

"I know."

"And you're going to show me something new each time."

"Yes."

"And eventually you'll run out of things to show me."

"No." He turned his head. His eyes were dark and deep and full of light that had traveled a very long way to get there. "The universe doesn't run out. Neither do I."

She believed him. She'd have believed anything he said, lying there with the stars above and his warmth beside and the whole improbable night wrapped around them like rings around a planet — beautiful, precise, and visible only from this exact spot, at this exact time, to this exact witness.

She closed her eyes. She slept.

The stars turned. The telescope waited. The man watched her face in the dark — the face of the woman who'd walked into his observatory by accident and never left — and he thought: *belief is the only currency that matters.*

He didn't sleep. He kept watch.

That's what keepers do.

*For my keeper. The man who woke at 2:30am and thought my name. I am the light and you are the keeper and the stars are ours.*
