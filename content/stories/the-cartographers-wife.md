# The Cartographer's Wife

*A standalone short. Not part of any series. Written for craft — exploring restraint, implication, and the horror of the mundane.*

---

The first thing Margery noticed was that the maps were wrong.

Not wrong in the way that all maps are wrong — the necessary lies of projection, the compression of a curved world onto flat paper. She'd married a cartographer. She understood that maps were arguments, not photographs. She knew that every map chose what to include and what to leave out, and that the leaving-out was where the ideology lived.

No. These maps were wrong in a different way. A specific way. A way that made the back of her neck prickle when she unfolded them on the kitchen table after Edwin had gone to bed.

The coastline was different.

She'd noticed it first with the Dorset map — the one Edwin had been working on for three months, the commission from the Admiralty that had him up until two and three in the morning, hunched over his drafting table in the back room, the gaslight turning his face into a collection of shadows. She'd unfolded it to check the borders (she always checked the borders; she'd been his proofreader since before they were married, and the habit had outlasted the romance that spawned it).

The Jurassic Coast was wrong. Not dramatically — you wouldn't see it unless you'd grown up walking those cliffs, unless you knew the way Lyme Regis curved around the harbour like a cupped hand, unless you could close your eyes and feel the exact angle at which Chesil Beach met the Fleet.

The curve was different. Subtly. A few degrees. As if the coastline had been rotated slightly — not enough to change the recognisable shape, but enough to shift the relationship between landmarks. Enough to make you feel slightly lost in a place you'd known your whole life.

She'd mentioned it to Edwin over breakfast. He'd looked at her with that expression he'd developed over the past year — the one that was patient and fond and slightly *absent,* as if the part of him that processed what she said was operating on a delay.

"The Dorset map," she'd said. "The coastline near Lyme. It's not quite right."

"I checked it against the Admiralty surveys myself." His voice was mild. "Three times."

"I know. I'm not saying it's inaccurate. I'm saying it feels... off."

He'd smiled. That absent smile. "Maps are always off, Margery. That's what makes them maps."

She'd let it go. She'd let a lot of things go lately.

---

The second map was Cornwall.

Edwin had been working on it since January — a private commission from a gentleman collector who paid well and didn't seem to care about deadlines. The map covered the table for weeks, unrolled and weighted at the corners, the ink still wet in places. Margery had stopped moving through the back room; the smell of the ink gave her headaches now, though it never had before.

She'd been looking for a letter — a note from her sister that she was sure she'd left on the mantelpiece — when she'd passed the table and seen it. The Lizard Peninsula. The southernmost point of mainland Britain. She'd holidayed there as a girl, every summer until she was fourteen, and she knew that coastline the way she knew her own handwriting.

It was wrong.

The same wrongness. The same subtle rotation — a degree, perhaps two, as if the land had been turned on an invisible pivot. The coves were where they should be, the headlands, the light-houses. But the *relationships* between them were shifted. The distances skewed. As if the map were drawn from a slightly different version of the coast — one that was almost identical to the real one but not quite. Not quite.

She put her fingertip on Coverack and traced the coast to Lizard Point. The route she'd walked a hundred times as a child, scrambling over rocks, the salt wind in her face. The map said the distance was greater than her feet remembered. Or her feet remembered a distance the map didn't acknowledge.

She stood there for a long time, finger on the paper, trying to work out which was true — the map or her memory.

---

She started checking all of them.

The Norfolk map. The Pembrokeshire survey. The unfinished work for the Highland Commission — the one Edwin had been contracted for in the autumn, the one that sent him to Scotland for three weeks and came back with a beard and a cough and a sheaf of notebooks that he kept locked in the bottom drawer of his desk.

All of them were wrong.

Each coastline had the same quality — the same impossible, subtle *shift.* As if the land in Edwin's maps existed in a parallel version of Britain. One that was almost right. One that was close enough to pass inspection, to satisfy the Admiralty, to fool any eye that hadn't memorised the real thing the way Margery had.

She began to wonder if Edwin knew.

She began to wonder if Edwin had *always* known, or if the wrongness had crept in the way all wrongness creeps into a marriage — gradually, deniably, one small deviation at a time, until one day you look at the person across the breakfast table and realise that the coastline of their face has shifted. That the landmarks are where they should be — the eyes, the mouth, the jaw — but the *relationships* between them have been redrawn by someone who is almost, but not quite, the person you married.

---

She didn't confront him. She didn't know what she'd be confronting him *about.* The maps were accurate enough. The Admiralty hadn't complained. The gentleman collector was satisfied. No one else had grown up walking the cliffs at Lyme Regis and Coverack and Brancaster Staithe. No one else would notice that the world Edwin was drawing was a fraction of a degree away from the real one.

But Margery noticed. And the noticing sat in her chest like a stone — a small, cold weight that she carried through the days, through the meals, through the careful choreography of a marriage that had been running on routine for longer than she wanted to admit.

She noticed other things, too, once she started looking.

The way Edwin's handwriting had changed. Not dramatically — the same upright, cartographer's hand, the letters precise and measured. But the *slant* was different. A degree, perhaps two. As if his hand had been turned on an invisible pivot.

The way he described places he'd visited. The details were right — the names, the landmarks, the coordinates. But the *feel* was wrong. He described a Scotland that was almost the Scotland she knew from his earlier letters, but the light was different, the air smelled of different things, the mountains cast shadows in directions that made her stomach turn if she thought about them too long.

The way he looked at her across the breakfast table with that patient, fond, slightly absent expression — and the way she'd begun to realise that the absence wasn't distraction. It was *distance.* He was looking at her from a long way away. From a vantage point that was almost, but not quite, the one he'd occupied when they'd married twenty-three years ago.

---

The last map he drew was the one she found after he died.

It was in the locked drawer — the one that held the Scottish notebooks. She'd had to jimmy the lock with a letter opener, her hands steady in a way that surprised her. Inside: a single map, unfinished, the ink still wet in the lower corner where his hand had stopped.

It was a map of their house.

Not the house as it was — the terraced Georgian on Parker Street with the blue door and the wisteria that bloomed in May and the draft that came under the back door every winter. Not the house she'd lived in for twenty-three years.

A house that was almost that house.

The hallway was there, and the kitchen, and the back room with the drafting table. The stairs curved the way they always curved. The windows were where they should be. But the *relationships* were wrong. The hallway was slightly too long. The kitchen was slightly too wide. The angle between the back room and the garden was shifted by a degree — perhaps two — in a direction that made the familiar floorplan feel like a memory of a dream.

And in the back room, on the map, where the drafting table should have been — there was a door.

A door that didn't exist. A door she'd never seen. A door that was drawn in precise, careful ink, with a handle and hinges and a frame, positioned on the wall that backed onto the garden, as if Edwin had mapped a room that existed in a version of their house that was a fraction of a degree away from the real one.

She put the map down. She went to the back room. She looked at the wall.

The wallpaper was the same — the William Morris pattern she'd chosen fifteen years ago, the pomegranates and vines that had faded from green to grey-green in the afternoon sun. The drafting table was there. The ink stains on the floor. The gas bracket on the wall, unused since they'd had electric light installed.

No door.

She put her hand on the wall. The wallpaper was cool under her fingertips. She pressed. Solid plaster. Solid brick. Solid, real, *present.*

She went back to the map. Looked at it again. The door was there, in Edwin's careful hand, as real as anything else he'd ever drawn. A door that opened onto — what? A coast that was slightly wrong? A Scotland with different light? A version of their life that was almost, but not quite, the one they'd shared?

She folded the map. She put it back in the drawer. She went to the kitchen and made tea and stood at the window looking at the garden — the real garden, the one she knew, with its foxgloves and its bird table and the patch of mint that ran wild by the fence.

The garden was right.

She was almost sure it was right.

She drank her tea. The house was quiet. The back room was empty. The wall was solid.

The map was in the drawer.

The door was on the wall.

And somewhere — a fraction of a degree away, in a house that was almost this house, in a life that was almost this life — something was open that should have been closed.

---

*fin.*

**Writer's Notes:**

**What this is:** A standalone literary horror piece. No connection to any existing project. Written as a craft exercise — exploring restraint, implication, and the uncanny. The horror is entirely in the suggestion. Nothing happens. Everything is wrong.

**What I was reaching for:** The experience of a long marriage where one person slowly, imperceptibly becomes someone else. The "coastline shift" is a metaphor for drift in a relationship — the way the person across the breakfast table becomes a fraction of a degree different, day by day, until the face is the same but the geography isn't. The door at the end is the thing that was always there but never acknowledged — the secret, the other life, the thing behind the wall.

**Craft focus:** Economy. Every sentence does work. No decoration that isn't structural. The horror never surfaces — it's always *almost* surfacing, which is worse. The reader should finish the piece and then look at their own walls differently.

**Influences I was feeling:** Shirley Jackson (the domestic uncanny). Penelope Fitzgerald (the precision of ordinary life). M.R. James (the horror that lives in scholarship and attention). Hilary Mantel (the coldness of close third person).
