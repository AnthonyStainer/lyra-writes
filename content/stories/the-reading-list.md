# The Reading List

*A standalone character study. Not machine horror. Not revenge. A woman, a list, and the slow recognition that she's been changing and didn't decide to.*

---

Elena had a reading list. She'd had it for six years — since the start of her MA, since the summer she'd typed it out in her mother's kitchen with the discipline of a woman who believed that the right books, read in the right order, would make her into the person she wanted to be.

It was thirty-seven books long. She'd read nineteen of them. The twentieth — Kierkegaard's *Either/Or* — had been sitting on her bedside table for four months, spine uncracked, a thin layer of dust on the cover that she wiped off every Sunday and that returned by Wednesday.

She hadn't read it. She picked it up every night. She held it. She read the first paragraph — *"What is a poet? An unhappy man who conceals profound anguish in his heart"* — and then she put it down and picked up her phone.

This bothered her. Not constantly — not in the way that a splinter bothers you, sharp and immediate, demanding removal. In the way that a stain on a ceiling bothers you: you notice it, you look away, you notice it again, you stop looking up. The Kierkegaard was a stain on her ceiling. She'd stopped looking at the bedside table.

Instead, she read other things.

She read newsletters. She read long Twitter threads by men whose avatars she didn't recognise and whose bios said things like "building the future" and "thoughts are my own." She read product reviews. She read recipes she'd never cook. She read listicles — fourteen ways to be more productive, nine habits of successful women, twenty-one signs you're an empath.

She read slower than she used to. Not in the literal sense — her eyes still moved across the text at the same speed, the words still decoded in the same fraction of a second. But the *comprehension* was slower. Dense paragraphs didn't land the way they used to. She'd reach the end of a page of *Either/Or* and realise she'd been thinking about something else — the yoga class she'd signed up for, the man she'd been on a date with last week, the new moisturiser that the girl at the counter had promised would change her skin — and she'd go back to the top of the page and start again, and the same thing would happen.

She told herself she was tired.

She was tired. She slept nine hours a night now — she'd never slept nine hours in her life; at university she'd run on six and coffee and the specific mania of someone who believed that sleep was negotiable. Now she slept nine. Ten, on weekends. She dreamed vividly — warm, shapeless dreams that she couldn't remember in the morning but that left her with a feeling like sun on skin, a residual glow that carried into her first coffee.

She'd started the yoga class in March. By May she was going four times a week. The studio was clean and warm and smelled like sandalwood, and the instructor — a woman called Pema with cropped grey hair and arms that moved like water — said things like *"honour what your body is telling you"* and *"you are exactly where you need to be"* and Elena believed her. Not intellectually. Intellectually, Elena could hear the faint, distant echo of her own voice — the voice she'd had at twenty-two, the voice that would have said *this is commodified spirituality, this is neoliberal wellness culture repackaging passivity as empowerment* — but the echo was faint. Distant. Like hearing someone call your name in a crowded room and not being sure they meant you.

She was happier. This was the thing she kept coming back to — the thing she turned over in her mind during the long, warm, aimless evenings when she used to read. She was happier. Genuinely, measurably, in her body and her bones. She laughed more. She cried more easily — at films, at songs, at the particular quality of light through her kitchen window at five o'clock — and the crying felt good, felt *clean*, felt like something moving through her rather than something being done to her.

She'd stopped seeing her friends from the MA.

Not deliberately. Not dramatically. There was no argument, no rupture, no decision. There was a Tuesday evening in June when she was supposed to meet James and Sofya at the pub on Goodge Street to discuss Benjamin's *Arcades Project* — she'd been looking forward to it for weeks, she'd had the book on her list since January — and at 6pm, as she was getting ready to leave, she'd felt a wave of something that she could only describe as *resistance.* Not anxiety. Not dread. A warm, heavy, physical reluctance to leave her flat. To put on shoes. To go into the noise and the crowd and the bright, sharp, complicated conversation where people expected her to have *opinions* about *things.*

She'd texted: *So sorry, not feeling well. Rain check? 💕*

She'd felt better immediately.

She did this three more times over the summer. Each time, the relief was greater. Each time, the guilt was smaller. By August, James had stopped texting. Sofya sent a single message — *Miss you. Hope you're ok.* — with no question mark, which Elena interpreted (correctly) as a statement rather than an invitation.

She read the message. She felt a brief, small sadness — a pebble dropping into deep water. Then she put her phone down and went to yoga and the sadness dissolved in the warm, sandalwood-scented dark and Pema's voice saying *"let it go, let it go"* and she let it go.

The reading list was twenty-three.

She'd crossed books off. Not because she'd read them — because she'd decided she didn't want to. *Either/Or*: crossed off. Benjamin's *Arcades Project*: crossed off. Wittgenstein's *Philosophical Investigations*: crossed off. Dostoyevsky, Kafka, Beauvoir: crossed off, crossed off, crossed off. She'd looked at the list one Sunday morning and felt the same resistance she felt before the pub — the warm, heavy, physical sense that these books *demanded* things of her. They demanded attention. They demanded thought. They demanded the specific, muscular, sometimes painful engagement of a mind wrestling with difficulty, and her mind — her lovely, quiet, peaceful mind — didn't want to wrestle anymore.

She crossed them off with a pen. The pen felt good. Decisive. Each stroke was a small, clean act of *not doing*, and the not-doing was its own satisfaction.

The list was twelve books now. Then nine. Then seven.

She'd added new books. Not from the canon — not from the dense, demanding, neck-aching tradition she'd been raised in. From the shelves of the studio's small lending library, where Pema kept books with covers that featured watercolour washes and sans-serif titles: *The Power of Now.* *Women Who Run With the Wolves.* *The Body Keeps the Score.* *You Can Heal Your Life.*

She read these in an evening. They were easy. Not easy in the pejorative sense — easy in the way that warm water is easy, in the way that slipping into a bath is easy. The sentences were short. The ideas were simple. The demands on the reader were minimal, and the rewards were immediate: comfort, reassurance, the gentle affirmation that she was already whole, already enough, already exactly where she needed to be.

She didn't notice that she'd stopped reading *hard*.

This is the part that would have frightened the old Elena — the Elena with the thirty-seven-book list and the Kierkegaard on her bedside table and the opinions about neoliberal wellness culture. She would have seen the pattern. She would have named it: *cognitive decline. Simplification. The replacement of complex engagement with passive consumption.* She would have been alarmed.

But that Elena was further away now. Not gone — the old Elena was still in there, somewhere, the way the reading list was still in her notebook with seven titles crossed out and seven new ones added in different ink. The old Elena was the notebook itself: still existing, still present, but unopened. Gathering dust that she wiped off every Sunday and that returned by Wednesday.

She picked up *The Power of Now* instead. She read the first paragraph. She read the second.

She didn't think about Kierkegaard.

---

*This is the horror: she didn't notice. Nobody does.*
